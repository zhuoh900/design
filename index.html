Furniture as social argument — who is this designed for?
01 — The Question

What lies beneath
the surface?

When a chair is designed, decisions are made: who can sit in it, for how long, at what cost, made by whose hands, from whose land. These are not aesthetic decisions. They are social ones.

Design operates at scales far beyond the object itself. It shapes bodies, organizes labor, consumes resources, signals belonging, and encodes values invisibly into the world. This project makes those scales visible.

02 — Impact at Every Scale

The object is just
the beginning.

01 — Body
Who does it fit?

Ergonomics is never neutral. The task chair designed for a 1960s male office worker still sets the global standard — encoding assumptions about who the "default" user is, often excluding women, disabled people, and non-Western body types.

02 — Household
Who does the work?

Objects organize domestic life and domestic labor. Furniture design shapes how families share space, distribute work, and perform identity — reflecting and reinforcing social norms about gender and class.

03 — Community
Who belongs here?

Public seating and communal objects define who is welcome in a space and who is designed out. "Hostile architecture" makes visible what is often done invisibly — by design.

04 — Planet
Who pays the cost?

Every object has a material history: extraction, production, transport, use, disposal. The ecological cost of everyday design is borne unevenly — by communities near mines, factories, and landfills.

03 — Case Studies

Objects with
arguments.

Case Study 01 — Seating
The Chair Is Never Just a Chair

From the throne to the office chair, seating encodes hierarchy. The task chair designed for a 1960s male office worker — still the global standard — causes chronic pain for bodies it was never designed for.

Inclusive seating design isn't just more comfortable. It's a political statement: that all bodies deserve to work without pain.

Body · Labor · Community
Case Study 02 — Tools
Who Designs the Hammer?

Standard tools were designed by and for men in formal trade settings. The rise of DIY culture and changing workforces revealed systemic exclusions — from grip size to weight distribution.

Redesigning tools is redesigning access to skilled work, home ownership, and economic self-sufficiency.

Body · Labor · Economy
Case Study 03 — Lifecycle
The Cost of "Affordable" Design

Flat-pack, low-cost furniture democratized interior design — but at a cost. Designed to be replaced rather than repaired, it generates enormous waste and depends on hidden labor exploitation across supply chains.

Who pays for cheap design? Workers in factories, communities near landfills, and the environment itself.

Central argument of this research project

The Design
Argument
is Public.

Every object is a decision made on behalf of someone. This project argues that making those decisions legible — their scale, their consequences, their politics — is the most powerful thing design communication can do.

Design is not decoration. It is governance — the quiet authority that decides who sits comfortably, who works safely, whose labor is invisible, and whose land is consumed. Making that visible is a political act.
